The Problem

What does it mean to be food-insecure? People who are food insecure lack consistent and affordable access to food. To put it in perspective, it could mean facing immense pressure and anxiety to find where the next meal is coming from. It could mean having to come back to your family and having to tell them there isn't going to be dinner tonight. And in the U.S., there are 44,000,000 people facing this exact situation.

SNAP, also known as food stamps, is the government’s largest nutrition benefit program. Yet the benefits only provide about $1.40 per person per meal, which is not enough to provide healthy sustenance for any individual.

Not surprisingly, the COVID-19 pandemic further exacerbated the problem of food insecurity with the pandemic-induced economic difficulties and the disruption of the food supply chain. Loss of income from unemployment caused by the pandemic pushed already vulnerable families into more severe food insecurity.

We analyzed the Food Insecure and Food Stamp participant demographics from 2010 to 2022 in order to investigate the patterns that might correlate to food insecurity and to highlight populations that need more support.

Food Insecurity Prevalence and SNAP benefits by State

Food insecurity rates in the USA vary widely among states, depending on the characteristics of their populations, availability of social services, and economic health. Each state has its own mix of people with different incomes, education levels, and types of jobs. These factors can affect how vulnerable a community is to not having enough nutritious food. For example, states with a diverse population or specific economic challenges might face more difficulties in ensuring everyone has access to sufficient food. Households in the South and Midwest are more likely to experience food insecurity than those on the coasts.

Secondly, the policies that states put in place and their economic conditions also play a big role. States with strong social safety nets and supportive policies can help protect people from food insecurity during tough times. The most food-insecure state following the impacts of COVID-19 is Mississippi. An important factor that contributed to Mississippi’s high food insecurity is the fact that the state of Mississippi did not extend emergency SNAP allotments through September 2020 (peak Covid period), a choice that put more families with burdened budgets at risk of hunger in the coming years.

The SNAP benefits chart clearly depicts some discrepancies for the highly disadvantaged southern states where the benefits do not reach the food insecure population completely. Still over the years of 2010 to 2022, there is a clear trend that food insecurity is becoming less prevalent.

You may wonder whom food stamps help. Most people would assume they go to households below the poverty line, right?

The graph below shows the percentage of food stamps that go to households below and above the poverty line per state per year. Hover on any state to see particular details of a year. You can see both the yearly cross-state differences and in-state differences.



Many states have an upward tick towards the end of their corresponding graph, which signifies the number of people below the poverty line receiving food stamps increased. Employment Status and Disability

Those who benefit from SNAP typically receive low wages, experience higher rates of unemployment, and have less employment stability. Month-to-month employment and hours worked are highly variable among those potentially affected by a SNAP work requirement. While two-thirds of participants are children, elderly, and people with disabilities, who are not expected to work, SNAP also helps workers, both to supplement low wages and support them when they are between jobs. The backdrop of frequent job turnover among low-income workers can make it difficult to assess and quantify the work experiences of SNAP recipients.

Americans living with disabilities face many obstacles in their everyday lives, including higher than average rates of unemployment, lower than average incomes, and costly medical expenses. On top of these obstacles, food insecurity disproportionately affects people with disabilities. The problem of food insecurity among households with people with disabilities would be far worse if not for food stamps. Conclusion

The biggest differences in SNAP benefits by state are primarily a result of the economic circumstances of each individual participant and the state in which they reside, rather than anything really inherent to the program itself. Residents of states with high poverty rates are naturally going to need SNAP more than others, and the program is geared more toward keeping people afloat rather than tackling the systematic issues that allow poverty to exist.

SNAP has proved a major resource for millions of Americans in need, and it isn't the only nutrition assistance program available. Despite all of this, as reported by the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities in 2021, researchers have estimated that SNAP benefits are approximately $11 per person below the amount needed to pay for a weekly nutritious meal plan.

This is especially apparent in higher-cost areas, where healthy diets are particularly hard to afford for low-income families due to a higher cost of living. The fact that SNAP can be more effective at counteracting poverty in some states than others shows that more needs to be done to ensure our most economically vulnerable citizens are provided for and protected.
